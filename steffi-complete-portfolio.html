How I Work

Principles that guide how I build

01

Ship fast, but don't ship shit

Speed is important, but quality matters more. I'd rather take an extra day to ship something solid than rush garbage into production. Fast iteration only works if you're building the right thing.

02

Data informs, intuition decides

I love metrics. I build dashboards. But data tells you what happened, not what to do next. The best decisions come from data + experience + understanding people.

03

If you can't explain it simply, it's not ready

Complexity is easy. Clarity is hard. If I can't explain a feature to someone who doesn't work in tech, I haven't thought through it enough.

04

Build for the problem, not the tech

The coolest tech stack doesn't matter if it doesn't solve a real problem. I've seen too many projects fail because we fell in love with the solution before understanding the problem.

05

Empathy scales better than code

Building financial literacy for 500+ families taught me: understanding people is harder than understanding systems. The best products come from deep empathy, not just good engineering.

Production Anomaly Detection @ Enact

↓

The Problem

Data team spending 20+ hours/week manually checking pipelines. Issues discovered hours after they occurred. No systematic way to catch anomalies early.

My Approach

Built statistical bounds + IQR methodology for real-time monitoring. Created Streamlit dashboards. Integrated Microsoft Teams alerting. Tested on historical data to tune thresholds.

The Outcome

Deployed to production serving enterprise data pipelines. 20+ hours/week saved. False positives down 40%. System accuracy at 98%. Still running today.

What I Learned

Production systems need to be boring and reliable. The fanciest algorithm doesn't matter if engineers don't trust it. Spent more time on UX/alerting than the detection logic.

Would I Do It Differently?

Yes - I'd involve data engineers earlier in the design process. Their feedback improved the final product significantly. Also would have built better documentation from day one.

Consumer Investment Product @ Finnarch

↓

The Problem

Serving customers with $50K+ portfolios but product wasn't sticky. High support volume. Users couldn't find features they needed.

My Approach

Conducted user interviews. Mapped current journey. Identified top 3 pain points. Defined requirements. Worked with engineering to ship iteratively.

The Outcome

Shipped 5 features over 3 months. Support tickets down. User satisfaction up. Most importantly: users actually used the features we built.

What I Learned

Talk to users before writing code. Obvious in retrospect, but easy to skip when you're moving fast. Also learned: ship small, ship often beats shipping everything at once.

Would I Do It Differently?

I'd set up better analytics from the start. We shipped features but couldn't measure impact as precisely as I wanted. Would have instrumented everything day one.

Smart Model Router (Side Project)

↓

The Problem

Using expensive models for simple queries. Wasting money and time. Needed intelligent routing based on complexity.

My Approach

Built classifier to detect query complexity. Route simple queries to fast/cheap models. Route complex queries to powerful models. Measure cost + latency improvements.

The Outcome

35% cost reduction. 50% latency improvement. Maintained quality. Open-sourced on GitHub. Others are using it.

What I Learned

Not every problem needs the most powerful tool. Sometimes the best solution is knowing when to use the simple thing. Also: sharing your work leads to unexpected opportunities.

Would I Do It Differently?

I'd add better monitoring earlier. Currently tracks cost/latency but could instrument quality metrics better. Would help understand trade-offs more precisely.

Problems I Want to Solve

Research questions that keep me up at night

How do we design systems that work at scale?

Most infrastructure fails when it needs to serve millions. What patterns emerge from systems that actually scale? How do technical constraints shape product decisions?

What makes products sticky vs. products that grow?

Some products keep users coming back. Others acquire users fast but lose them faster. What's the difference? Can we predict retention from early signals?

How do we measure what actually matters?

Traditional metrics optimize for the wrong outcomes. What frameworks help us measure long-term value? How do we balance quantitative data with qualitative insight?

Where does AI genuinely create value?

Beyond the hype, where does AI solve real problems vs. automate busywork? What categories benefit most from intelligence augmentation? How do we build AI products people trust?

How do we bridge technical depth and business impact?

Engineers understand what's possible. Business understands what's valuable. How do we connect these worlds? What skills and frameworks help translate between them?

What patterns predict marketplace success?

Two-sided marketplaces are hard to build. What determines whether they reach liquidity? How do successful platforms solve the cold start problem? What early metrics actually matter?
